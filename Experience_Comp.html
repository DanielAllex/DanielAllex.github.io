ACSL

The American Computer Science League is a Computer Science competition where students answer questions on various computer science topics and complete programming long answer problems. For my first time competing in the ACSL, I competed in the Intermediate division. I received the full points for all of the programming parts for all 4 contests, and I learned about many different topics that are tested in the ACSL. I qualified for the Finals competition, and I placed within the top 26% of all qualifiers in my division. For my senior year, I plan to compete in the Senior Division. More about this competition including the tested topics can be found on this page.

Twenty Four Seven Grid Puzzle

The website janestreet.com/puzzles gives monthly puzzles for people to solve, some of which can only realistically be done through programming. I decided to complete one from 2018 called Twenty Four Seven specifically due to its large reliance on programming. Not only did I have to implement logic with randomization in order to have my compiler solve this puzzle in a reasonable time, but I also had to implement an ACSL topic that I previously only used conceptually in problems. I used the concepts of Graph Theory to check whether all of the nonzero boxes were connected. I created an adjacency list and then used recursion to see whether it is possible to "travel" from the first box to every other box. In the end, my puzzle was logical enough to solve the puzzle in less than a minute at the fastest (some other solutions took the compiler hours or even days to solve).
