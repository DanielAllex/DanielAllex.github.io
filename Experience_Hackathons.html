Hack PHS

My first hackathon was at Princeton Highschool where I worked in a team to create Audio Alpha, a web app that would point out different flaws in speech such as pauses between words, grammar, basic vocabulary, and repeated words. The majority of what I worked on was checking grammar. This was my first time using Python, but I learned how to use natural language processing to turn words into their type of word, and based on that, I was able to create a grammar checker that corrected numerous types of errors (focusing on the types that were most common during speeches and presentations). In the end, we had an impressive backend, but our frontend was basic.

Hack TCNJ

For my next hackathon, I went to TCNJ. After our downfall with Audio Alpha was the lack of design, we put a larger emphasis on design and attempted to make a reimagination of Audio Alpha: Audiolytic. Audiolytic would have an interactive timeline and would have a completely new backend, having more features and having more accurate ways to compute these features. I worked on the home page and aspects of the analysis and timeline page. The homepage was completed, the timeline worked when manually entering data to display, and the backend was complete and could retrieve data from raw audio input, but the connection between the frontend and the backend had an unexpected bug that ultimately led to us not finishing. As a result, we did not submit our project, but I feel that this hackathon greatly improved my HTML, CSS, and JavaScript skills.

NotUniversity Hacks

With the pandemic going on at the time, I had to attend an online hackathon rather than a physical one. For this hackathon, I worked on a chrome extension that would highlight misinformation. I worked on the actual chrome extension part, that sent the text on a page to a Python server, to then after receive a list of incorrect sentences from the Python server. Using this list, I made it so that the misinformation was highlighted. My group created a database for misinformation using MongoDB based on websites that fact check information. Although we did not win, at the time, this was our best hackathon because we actually completed a good project. We actually liked the idea so much that we decided to further develop the chrome extension on the side: we hope to automate the database, fix bugs, improve design, and eventually host the Python on a real server to then actually release the chrome extension to the Chrome Store.
